# Specific Risks and their Controls

## Wood Dust
### Exposure Activities
- Sanding, cutting, filing and removal of wood  

### Health Issues Associated with the Risk
- Very little exposure is required to trigger the adverse health effects of wood dust; if wood dust is noticeable in the air, it is well past the airborne concentrated needed to cause these effects.
- Eye, nose and throat irritation  
- Shortness of breath  
- Dryness and soreness of throat  
- Conjunctivitis   
- Rhinitis (runny nose)  
- Dermatitis (red, itchy, dry or blistery skin)    
- Decreased lung capacity  
- Allergic reactions and inflammation of the walls of the air sacs and small airways (hypersensitivity pneumonitis, which may develop within hours or days of exposure and often presents with cold or flu-like symptoms such as headaches, chills, sweating, nausea, breathlessness, etc., all of which can worsen with continued exposure.)  
- Occupational asthma (especially when working with Western Red Cedar)  
- Toxins in some species of wood  
  - Headaches, weight loss, breathlessness, giddiness, cramps, irregular heartbeat  
- Toxins in moulds that grow on and in the wood   
- Many of the risks are increased with woods that are more common in BC (Alder, Cedar, Firs, Hemlocks, Larch, Pine, Spruce and Yew)  

### Controls
- Particulate mask or respirator cartridges, rated as N95 or KN95.  
- Subsitute with a less hazardous species of wood  
- Reduce the number of cuts or processing steps required for the project  
- Use adequate ventilation, including HEPA filters  
- Use adequate dust control, including on-tool extraction systems  
- Wear appropriate PPE **if anyone in the shop is producing wood dust, regardless of whether you are the one completing the work**  
- **Do not** use compressed air to blow the dust off of equipment or clothing if at all possible  

### More Information
(Canadian Centre for Occupational Health and Safety)[https://www.ccohs.ca/oshanswers/chemicals/wood_dust.html#section-3-hdr]


## Oil-based (Solvent-based) Finishing Products
### Exposure Activities 
- Finishing furniture and wood projects  
- Sanding between coats  
- Removing previious finishes  
### Health Issues Associated with the Risk
- Slight to severe eye irritation (see specific product's SDS)  
- Slight to severe skin irritation (see specific SDS)  
- High gas, vapor, mist or dust concentrations may be harmful if inhaled  
- High vapor concentrations are irritating to the eyes, nose, throat and lungs  
- Excessive inhalation may cause irritation to the respiratory tract  
- May cause cancer  
- Flammable liquids and vapours  
- May cause genetic defects  
- Causes damage to organs through prolonged or repeated exposure  
- May cause allergic skin reactions, including reactions that result from repeated exposures in people without a history of reacting to the product  

### Controls
- Avoid breathing fumes, spray, vapors or mist  
- Avoid prolonged contact with product  
- Keep away from heat, hot surfaces, sparks and open flames as well as equipment with the potential to become hot or create sparks  
- Keep container tightly closed and replace lids whenever possible when working with the product  
- Wash hands thoroughly after handling, ideally with a soap that includes pumice  
- Wear protective gloves, clothing, eye protection and face protection
- If on skin or clothes: immediately remove contaminated clothing and rinse skin with water and soap
- In case of fire, use alcohol-film forming foam, carbon dioxides, dry  chemical, or **dry sand** to exinguish
- In case of fire, **notify 911 and firefighting personnel of the presence of hazardous materials and flammable/explosive chemicals**
- In case of fire, evacuate as far as possible to avoid the inhalation of toxic smoke and gases
- Dispose of product in accordance with local, regional and national regulations
- Do not expose food or drink to fumes or vapors; keep out of workplace or tightly sealed

## Water-based Finishing Products
### Exposure Activities
- Finishing furniture and wood projects  
- Sanding between coats  
- Removing previous finishes  
### Health Issues Associated with the Risk
- Slight skin and eye irritation  
- Some products contain slight amounts (around 5%) of ingredients that be toxic  
### Controls
- Wear nitrile gloves
- Clean-up with soap and water
- Wash hands with soap and water after use
- Launder clothes before re-wearing (can go in washer/dryer)
- Wear eye protection to prevent substances entering eyes
- Open windows and doors to provide cross-ventilation and avoid the build-up of fumes
### More Information
- Manufacturer SDS

## Wax-based and Non-solvent-based Oil Finishing Products
### Exposure Activities
- Finishing furniture and wood projects
- Sanding between coats
- Removing previous finishes
### Health Issues Associated with the Risk
- Generally, there are few/no known health risks associated with these products. **However**, it is best practice to use the safety precautions associated with water-based finishing products, unless otherwise specified by a product's labelling or SDS.  
- Always follow the specific instructions provided for the product you are using, and refer to its SDS.  
### Controls
- See controls recommended for water-based finishing products
