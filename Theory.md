# Basic Finishing Theory & Techniques

- Tape off glue-up areas

### GLOSSARY
**PPE**
- Personal Protection (or Protective) Equipment

**VOCs**
- Volitile Organic Compounds; the 'big bads' of hazardous finishing materials, these compounds may be completely odourless or may produce irritating fumes.

**OV**
- Oganic Vapours -- usually used when referring to respirator cartridges

### Why Do We Finish Wood?
- Helps to mitigate changes in moisture content that cause warping and instability  
- Can enable a wood surface to be waterproof!  
- Makes wood furniture and items much, much easier to keep clean  
- Can improve long-term health effects by sealing manufacturered wood products (hardboard, MDF, etc) so that they don't continue to leach harmful substances into the environment  
- Allows us to 'disguise' cheaper, more readily available, or more sustainable woods as more expensive, less sustainable, or less available woods  
- Allows us to make pieces that are more uniform visually, even if we've used different pieces of wood to make them; creates cohesion  
- Allows for the application of different colours or lustres to our finished items  
- Increases wear resistance and hardiness  
- Can turn a non-food-safe wood into a food-safe product (although I'm not **recommending** you do that)    
- Enhances the natural grain and colour of wood  

### How Do We Choose a Finish?
- Start at the beginning of the project!
  - The way a finish looks varies based on the species of wood it's applied to  
  - Some materials lend themselves to different treatments better than others; imagine the difference between painting a piece of flat MDF and painting a rough-cut piece of Douglas Fir  
  - The amount of pre-finishing you have to do may affect the starting dimensions of your wood; if you know that you're going to be wanting a mirror-like finish on your piece, you're going to be 'ramping up' through many, many grits of sandpaper and will be removing more material than if you wanted to produce a satin or matte finish  
  - Consider the effect you want, and it's durability -- is your piece destined to be a favoured heirloom toy chest, or a dining table for your children or grandkids', or will it be a beautiful clock sitting on a mantlepiece? Each of these requires a different approach.  
  - Safety considerations -- do you have the required environment and PPE to use solvent-based and spray finishes, or do you need to stick with water-based or oil-based ones that contain fewer VOCs?  
  - Time considerations -- how soon does your finish project need to be used; do you have a week to let the finish cure? How much time do you have to work on the piece -- can you use a multi-day process, or do you only have an afternoon?
  - Longevity -- does your project need to last for an event or a season (such as a sign), or does the investment you've spent in time and materials warrant a more long-lasting outcome?  
  - Skill -- how skilled are you at finishing? Will the finish you want to apply look the way you want it to without practice, or should you use an easier technique until you have more confidence?  
  - Cost -- some finishes are extremely expensive, while others are relatively cheap. Some finishes are designed for professionals and require you to buy a far larger volume than you'll ever use -- is it possible for you to maybe buy a smaller quantity off of a cabinet shop or woodworking friend, or are you okay with the cost associated with the finish you want to use? Is there a way to replicate a more expensive finish with a less expensive one?  
  - Preparation -- how much preparation is necessary for the finish you want to use? Are you patient enough to want to go through many tedious steps in order to achieve a desired outcome, or are you likely to give-up halfway through in exchange for 'good enough'?  
  - Food safety -- does your end result need to be food safe, or not? You'll need to know this before you start, because many varieties of wood are not food safe.

### Water-Based Finishes
Water-based finishes are relatively new to the playing field, and generally seek to replicate the durablity and ease-of-use that oil-based finishes (especially solvent-based ones) offer, but without the environmental and health risks. They dry quickly, don't have a strong smell and are cleaned up easily with soap and water. They also stay clear, rather than yellowing or developing a patina over time, and are easy to re-apply. Generally these finishes require more coats than oil-based products do, though there are an increasing number of dependable one-coat options on the market.

### Oil-Based Finishes
There are two main types of oil-based finishes: those that include solvents to help them penetrate the wood and increease the evenness of their application, and those that do not. Often, those that don't contain solvents are referred to as "natural oil" finishes, and include things such as Danish oil, teak oil, linseed oil, mineral oil and tung oil. Generally, these natural oils are very easy to apply, but they require time to cure before they reach their full potential for protection, lustre and colour, and they typically require spirits or solvents to clean up -- though that's changing as more and more products come on the market that can do the job without the safety implications of solvents. Natural oils also generally need multiple coats, but the end result is a finish that greatly enhances the wood's natural characteristics and has a depth to it.
#### Solvent-Based
Solvent-based finishes are oil-based finishes that have had a solvent added to them, either to change their behaviour or their method of application. Solvents can change a finish's behaviour by increasing its viscosity, allowing for a more even application and deeper, faster penetration into the wood. They can change their method of application by allowing them to be applied through an aerosol or in a 'fast drying' format. When solvents are added to a natural oil, they change the safety implications enough that you should consider the result a solvent-based product, rather than a natural one. For instance, while Danish oil is an easy-to-apply and generally safe natural finish, Danish oil in an aerosol format is typically solvent-based and requires much more ventilation and PPE.

### Wax-based Finishes
Along with oils, waxes are the oldest method of finishing wood products. The simplest is beeswax, but carnauba wax and modern wax pastes are also very popular options. Each option has different properties, and waxes are the most varied category of finish type. Typically, waxes leave a project feeling velvety, and with a low-sheen, though paste waxes have various formulations that can include gloss, and buffing wheels can allow even beeswax and carnauba waxes to achieve a near glass-like appearance and rigidity. Waxes are, however, the least hardy option for finishing -- but one of the easiest to re-apply and maintain long-term.

### Shellac
Shellac is an easy-to-apply finish that warms with age and dries quickly. However, it doesn't respond well to heat or water so it's best used for furniture (*with coasters*) or decorative pieces.

### Lacquer
Lacquer is a method of finishing wood that became popular around the 1500s-1700s and produces a high-gloss finish that gains a warm amber tone over time. It is generally what store-bought furniture is finished with, although it's increasingly being swapped out for polyurethanes. Lacquer is almost impossible to apply successfully without spraying, and requires considerable PPE.

### Polyurethane
Polyurethane, or "Poly", is the most durable finish available. A synthetic, plastic resin that forms a tough, protective layer over the wood, polyurethanes offer quick drying times (though longer cure times than other products), a clear appearance, a variety of lustres and a waterproof finish. It is the go-to for many woodworkers, especially for items that are expected to see a lot of wear-and-tear, heavy use, or which need to be waterproof and heat-resistant.
  
### Applicators 
- There are many different applicators and while a large number of them advertise that they're 'universal' or can work with a large variety of finishes, there are definitely better applicator-finish pairings than others...
- Generally, the success of a finish will depend on the quality of the materials used. If you have a fixed budget, splurge on a better applicator rather than on a better finishing product. Buy the highest quality applicator you can afford.

#### Water-Based Finishes
**Use**
- Synthetic brushes and blends (nylon, polyester)  
  - Resist water absorbtion and hold shape  
  - Look for thicker, denser brushes  
- Microfiber or woven roller covers  
  - Minimize splatter, hold water-based finishes well, apply smoothly and generally don't drop lint  
- Microfiber stain pads

**Avoid**
- Natural bristle brushes  
  - These will absorb the water out of the finish, creating a sticky-gooey mess and affecting the way the finish applies to the surface  
- Foam brushes  
  - These will aerate the finish, creating bubbles and an inconsistent application of the finishing product   

#### Oil-Based Finishes
**Use**
- Natural bristle brushes
  - Excellent at retaining oil products, and the oil helps to maintain the brush's bristles as you go!
  - Condition your natural brush with some mineral spirits before use to help with cleaning and to get a more uniform load
- Foam brushes
  - Work well for staining, and for small areas where you want a smooth finish
- Mohair or lambswool roller covers
  - Expensive, but very reusable and will achieve a smooth finish

**Avoid**
- Most synthetic brushes unless they specifically state they're good for oil-based products
  - Solvents can break down the bristles
- Microfibre pads and rollers
  - Oil tends to 'gum' them up and cause them to drag across a surface

#### Polyurethanes
**Use**
- Light pressure and long, even strokes
- Dense brushes, with compact bristles
- Angled brush tips

#### Stains
**Use**
- Lint-free rags and pads
- Even strokes worked over a small area

#### When in Doubt
- Most finishing products will specify an appropriate applicator on their label  
- Staff at finishing supply stores (Home Depot, Windsor Plywood, Paint stores, etc.) will generally be able to recommend appropriate pairings  

### The Golden Rule
- Always apply **oil-based sealers over oil-based stains and paints** and **water-based sealers over water-based stains and paints**
- If you choose one product to apply to your project and it's water-based, every other product you select (wood conditioners, stains, sealers, primers, etc.) should be water-based, and vice versa with oil.

### Step 1: Achieving an Adequately Prepared Surface
- Depending on the amount of gloss you want your finished surface to have, you will want to go through a progression of sandpaper grits
  - Each grit replaces the scratches of the last grit, and because each progressive grit creates tighter scratch patterns, the scratch patterns start to 'disappear' both to our eye and to the touch
  - Generally, we want to sand to at least 220 grit before applying any finishing products; sanding to a higher grit than that is generally pointless at this stage
  - Between applications of the chosen finish, we want to increase our grit. So we'll start with 320 (if doing 3 or more coats) or 400 (if doing 2 coats) and then move on from there, generally to around 600 grit.
- There are some notable alternatives to sanding:
  - Hand planing
  - Cabinet scrapers
  - Very fine steel wool, which burnishes the surface but is a lot of hard work
- On highly figured wood, sanding does not treat winter wood and summer wood evenly, so you'll be left with microscopic high areas and low areas (or noticeable ones...)
  - To compensate for this, you can spray the surface of the wood with enough water that the surface begins to swell, then leave it to dry and sand it down. Because the winter wood will swell differently from the summer wood, you'll be able to sand them back to a 'common denominator' level.
- After sanding, planing, scraping or burnishing, wood should be cleaned using an eco-friendly TSP (or regular TSP, with appropriate PPE, ventilation and disposal) or a wood cleaner that does not leave a residue; you can also use mineral spirits

### Step 3: Curing
- All finishes require time to 'cure' -- though some show it more than others. It's a good idea to leave your project for a minimum of 1-3 days after your final finishing application in order to let that finish harden on a molecular level, before subjecting it to any use. I also recommend not placing it in its final location just yet, too -- in the curing process, finishes can sometimes get sticky before they get hard, and it's no fun to be scraping half-cured finish off the beloved piece of furniture the newly completed project is sitting on.
